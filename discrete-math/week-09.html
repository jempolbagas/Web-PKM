1 Why Does This Matter?

Here's a question that will follow you through all of computer science: how many steps does this algorithm take? It sounds simple, but answering it precisely requires a tool we don't yet have — a way to mathematically describe what loops accumulate. That tool is the discrete series.

Think about the last time you wrote a nested for-loop. You probably had an intuitive sense that the inner loop "does more work" as the outer index grows. But can you say exactly how much more? Can you write a single formula — no loops, no counting — that tells you the total number of operations for any input size $n$? That's precisely what this week teaches you to do.

Beyond algorithms, series are everywhere in applied mathematics. The compound interest formula your bank uses is a geometric series in disguise. Signal processing relies on infinite series to decompose and reconstruct waveforms. Even the famous Fibonacci sequence, which we'll see again in Week 10, has a closed-form expression derived through series techniques. This week is your entry point into that world.

? Quick check — before reading further: can you guess what $1 + 2 + 3 + \ldots + 100$ equals, and why there's a fast way to calculate it?

The sum is 5050. The elegant trick — attributed to Gauss — is to pair terms from opposite ends: $(1 + 100) + (2 + 99) + \ldots + (50 + 51) = 50 \times 101 = 5050$. More generally, $\sum_{i=1}^{n} i = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$. This is the closed-form arithmetic series formula — one of the most important results in this week's material.

3 Arithmetic Progressions

The Intuition

An arithmetic progression (AP) is just a sequence where you add the same number each time. Start at 3 and keep adding 5: $3, 8, 13, 18, 23, \ldots$ The number you keep adding is called the common difference, $d$. The sequence is perfectly regular — no surprises, no branching — just a steady march forward.

The interesting question isn't about individual terms, but about their sum. If you had to add up the first $n$ terms of an AP, you could do it term by term — but that's $O(n)$ work. The algebraic formula lets you do it in $O(1)$, regardless of how large $n$ gets.

Definition
Arithmetic Progression — Finite Sum

Given a first term $a$ and common difference $d$, the $n$-th term is $a_n = a + (n-1)d$.

The sum of the first $n$ terms is: $$S_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl(a + (i-1)d\bigr) = \frac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$$

This can also be written as $S_n = \frac{n}{2}(a_1 + a_n)$, i.e., the number of terms times the average of the first and last term.

Worked Example Sum of a 6-term arithmetic progression

Problem: Find the sum of the sequence $2, 5, 8, 11, 14, 17$.

1
Identify the parameters. First term $a = 2$, common difference $d = 3$, number of terms $n = 6$.
2
Apply the formula. $$S_6 = \frac{6}{2}(2 \cdot 2 + (6-1) \cdot 3) = 3(4 + 15) = 3 \times 19 = 57$$
3
Verify by counting. $2 + 5 + 8 + 11 + 14 + 17 = 57$. ✓
⚠️ Common mistake: Forgetting that the formula gives the sum of the first $n$ terms starting from $a_1 = a$. If the sequence starts at a different index (e.g., $a_3$), you need to adjust accordingly.
? An arithmetic sequence starts at $a = 5$ with common difference $d = -2$. What is $S_{10}$ (the sum of the first 10 terms)?

Using $S_n = \frac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$:

$$S_{10} = \frac{10}{2}(2 \cdot 5 + (10-1)(-2)) = 5(10 - 18) = 5 \times (-8) = -40$$

Notice that a negative $d$ means the series eventually goes negative and the sum is pulled down. The sequence is: $5, 3, 1, -1, -3, -5, -7, -9, -11, -13$. Sum $= -40$. ✓

4 Geometric Progressions

The Intuition

A geometric progression (GP) is like an AP's more dramatic cousin. Instead of adding a constant, you multiply by a constant each time. Start at 1 and keep doubling: $1, 2, 4, 8, 16, \ldots$ The multiplier is the common ratio, $r$.

GPs grow (or shrink) exponentially — which is exactly why they're so important in computer science. The total work done by a divide-and-conquer algorithm (where each level spawns $r$ subproblems) follows a geometric series. The number of nodes in a complete binary tree is a geometric sum.

And here's where GPs become genuinely mysterious: what happens when we never stop? If $r$ is small enough — specifically, if $|r| < 1$ — then the terms shrink towards zero fast enough that even an infinite sum converges to a finite number. This is one of the most important ideas in all of analysis, and it starts right here.

Definition
Geometric Progression — Finite Sum

Given a first term $a$ and common ratio $r \neq 1$, the sum of the first $n$ terms is: $$S_n = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} a \cdot r^i = a \cdot \frac{1 - r^n}{1 - r}$$

If $r = 1$, then all terms equal $a$ and $S_n = na$.

Theorem
Infinite Geometric Series — Convergence

If $|r| < 1$, the infinite series converges: $$S_\infty = \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} a \cdot r^i = \frac{a}{1 - r}$$

If $|r| \geq 1$, the series diverges — it has no finite sum.

Why Does the Finite Formula Work?

Rather than just memorizing the formula, let's derive it — because the derivation reveals a beautiful algebraic trick you'll see again and again. The key idea is telescoping: multiply the sum by $r$ and subtract.

Derivation Proof of the finite geometric sum formula
1
Write out the sum: $S_n = a + ar + ar^2 + \ldots + ar^{n-1}$
2
Multiply both sides by $r$: $rS_n = ar + ar^2 + \ldots + ar^{n-1} + ar^n$
3
Subtract: $S_n - rS_n = a - ar^n$. Notice how all the middle terms cancel — that's the telescoping trick.
4
Factor and divide: $S_n(1 - r) = a(1 - r^n)$, so $S_n = \dfrac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$.  $\square$
? Does the series $\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^k$ converge? If yes, what does it converge to?

Yes, it converges. The ratio is $r = \frac{2}{3}$, and $|r| = \frac{2}{3} < 1$, so the convergence condition is satisfied.

Using the infinite sum formula with $a = 1$ (the first term is $\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^0 = 1$): $$S_\infty = \frac{1}{1 - \frac{2}{3}} = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{3}} = 3$$

Intuition: even though there are infinitely many terms, they shrink so fast that they sum to exactly 3.

5 Translating Loops into Summations

This is the most practically useful skill in this week's material. The goal is to take a piece of code — a loop or nested loop — and write its total operation count as a sigma expression. Once it's in sigma form, you can apply the AP and GP formulas to get a closed-form answer.

The method is always the same: read the loop bounds, write the sum, collapse from the inside out.

Worked Example Translating a triangular nested loop

Problem: How many times does the inner body execute?

count = 0
for i in range(1, n + 1):      # i = 1, 2, ..., n
    for j in range(1, i + 1):  # j = 1, 2, ..., i
        count += 1              # ← count this
1
Write the double summation. The outer index $i$ runs from 1 to $n$; for each $i$, the inner index $j$ runs from 1 to $i$. Each inner iteration contributes 1 operation. $$\text{count} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{i} 1$$
2
Evaluate the inner sum. $\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{i} 1 = i$ (it's just counting from 1 to $i$, which gives $i$ steps). $$\text{count} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} i$$
3
Apply the AP formula. This is the sum of the first $n$ integers — a classic arithmetic series with $a=1$, $d=1$: $$\text{count} = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} = O(n^2)$$
⚠️ Common mistake: Writing the inner limit as $n$ instead of $i$. Always check — is the inner limit a constant, or does it depend on the outer loop variable? If it depends on $i$, the limits are not symmetric and require more care.
? Translate and solve: what is the closed-form of count in this loop?
for i in range(1, n+1): for j in range(1, 2*i): count += 1

The inner loop runs $j$ from 1 to $2i - 1$ (since Python's range(1, 2*i) stops before $2i$), giving $2i - 1$ iterations per outer step.

$$\text{count} = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(2i - 1) = 2\sum_{i=1}^{n}i - \sum_{i=1}^{n}1 = 2 \cdot \frac{n(n+1)}{2} - n = n^2$$

So the exact answer is $n^2$ — the total is a perfect square regardless of $n$. This is a known identity: the sum of the first $n$ odd numbers equals $n^2$.

6 Synthesis — How It All Connects

Arithmetic and geometric series aren't really separate topics — they're two points on a spectrum of how sequences can grow. The AP grows linearly: add a constant, accumulate a quadratic sum. The GP grows exponentially: multiply a constant, and depending on whether $r > 1$ or $r < 1$, you get either explosive growth or graceful decay.

What connects them to algorithm analysis is the loop translation skill. Every loop has an implicit summation hiding in it. Once you see that, you can apply the AP or GP formula directly to get a complexity result. Next week, we'll generalize this when we study recurrence relations — where the complexity of one call depends on the complexity of smaller calls, and we need even more powerful tools to unpack the sum.

Think of this week as building the vocabulary. You've learned to speak in sums. Starting next week, you'll learn to speak in equations about sums.

Problem 1 Level 1

Calculate the exact sum of the infinite geometric series: $$\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} 3 \cdot \left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^k$$

View Full Solution

Step 1 — Find the first term. The series starts at $k = 2$, so the first term is $a = 3 \cdot \left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 = \frac{3}{16}$.

Step 2 — Check convergence. $r = \frac{1}{4}$, and $|r| < 1$, so the series converges.

Step 3 — Apply the formula. $$S_\infty = \frac{a}{1-r} = \frac{\frac{3}{16}}{1 - \frac{1}{4}} = \frac{\frac{3}{16}}{\frac{3}{4}} = \frac{3}{16} \cdot \frac{4}{3} = \frac{1}{4}$$

Final Answer: The sum is $\dfrac{1}{4}$.

Problem 2 Level 2

Translate the following loop into a mathematical summation, simplify completely, and express the result as a closed-form formula in $N$: 

count = 0
for i in range(1, N + 1):
    for j in range(1, i * 2):
        count += 1
View Full Solution

Step 1 — Set up the summation. The inner loop runs $j$ from 1 to $2i - 1$, giving $2i - 1$ iterations for each value of $i$. $$\text{count} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j=1}^{2i-1} 1 = \sum_{i=1}^{N} (2i - 1)$$

Step 2 — Expand and apply standard identities. $$\sum_{i=1}^{N}(2i-1) = 2\sum_{i=1}^{N}i - \sum_{i=1}^{N}1 = 2 \cdot \frac{N(N+1)}{2} - N = N(N+1) - N = N^2$$

Final Answer: $\text{count} = N^2$. The algorithm is $\Theta(N^2)$.

Problem 3 Level 3 — Challenge

A computer performs a task that takes 1 second on the first day. Each subsequent day, it takes exactly half as long as the day before. If the computer runs this task every day forever, what is the total time spent? Then, derive what happens in the general case where the task takes $t_0$ seconds on day 1 and shrinks by a factor of $r$ ($0 < r < 1$) each day.

View Full Solution

Specific case. The daily times form an infinite geometric series with $a = 1$ and $r = \frac{1}{2}$: $$\text{Total} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 1 \cdot \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n = \frac{1}{1 - \frac{1}{2}} = 2 \text{ seconds}$$

General case. With first term $t_0$ and ratio $r \in (0, 1)$: $$\text{Total} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} t_0 r^n = \frac{t_0}{1 - r}$$

Insight: No matter how long the task runs, as long as it shrinks by a constant ratio each time, the total time is always finite: exactly $\frac{t_0}{1-r}$. When $r = \frac{1}{2}$, the total is always exactly $2t_0$.

Week 9 — Quick Reference Card
Key Formulas
  • AP Sum: $S_n = \frac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$
  • GP Finite: $S_n = \frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$
  • GP Infinite: $S_\infty = \frac{a}{1-r}$ when $|r|<1$
  • $\sum_{i=1}^{n} i$: $= \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$
  • $\sum_{i=1}^{n} (2i-1)$: $= n^2$
Key Definitions
  • Common difference $d$: Constant added between AP terms
  • Common ratio $r$: Constant multiplied between GP terms
  • Convergence: Infinite sum approaches a finite limit
  • Closed-form: A formula with no loops or sums — just arithmetic
⚠️ Never forget to check $|r| < 1$ before applying the infinite GP formula. If $|r| \geq 1$, the series diverges and the formula doesn't apply.
